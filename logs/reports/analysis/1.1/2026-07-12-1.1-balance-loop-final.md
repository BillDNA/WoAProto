# Balance loop v2 — final report (rules 1.1)

_Run 2026-07-12, between sprints. **Cards as the only variable knob** — maps (`core7`), rules (1.1), unit
values and AI weights frozen throughout, so every row below is apples-to-apples. 3 iterations · **6 deck
slots** · **4,200 battles** (7 maps × 100, hard-vs-hard, `--once` fixed seed schedule) · 1 seed-1001
LLM feels-match. Noise band ±100/√700 ≈ **±3.8 pts** fleet-wide._

**Bottom line for Bill — two things, and the second is the important one:**

1. **There is a deck to ship.** `cavsplit17-raid-paid` passes **every** north star at **T0 (strict)** and
   beats baseline on both goals you named — Drag 2.4 → **2.2**, Swings 2.8 → **3.3** — while killing the
   deck's worst auto-play (Deploy Cavalry 1stSight 79 → 20). It needs one ruling from you: it is a
   **17-card deck** (see §5b.3).
2. **The AI's evaluation function is strategically wrong, and the LLM match proved it.** `unitOnBoard: 22`
   vs `unitReserve: 16` tells the AI to deploy on sight. Two independent LLM players discovered by playing
   that **holding reserve and deploying late is what wins.** Both AI sides share the error so it cancels
   out in AI-vs-AI and the sweep looks clean — which is exactly why nothing caught it before. **This is
   the highest-leverage finding in the report** (§5a.1).

## 3. The story

**Iteration 1 — the game is healthy; the deck is what needs work.** All ten north stars pass on
`default`, and 700 battles independently re-validated the rules-1.1 guardrails. *Before* that could be
trusted the loop had to fix its ruler: the "baselines to protect" were still 0.x-era, and **attacks/swaps
had inverted between eras** (V0 4.9/6.5 → 1.1 6.1/5.7) — grading against them would have called healthy
decks broken.

**Then the loop turned on its own instrument, and four adversarial checkers dismantled the first
diagnosis.** Iteration 1 confidently reported an "empty middle band", a "deadest card" (Attack +1,
AvgSeen 10.85), and a 3-for-3 replacement batch. **All of it was artifact:**

- **`1stSight%` ≈ the AI's immediate eval delta.** `unitOnBoard: 22` vs `unitReserve: 16` hands every
  deploy step a free 6-point gap × unit value — that one number rank-orders the whole deploy family
  (cav ×2 = +48 → 79%; art = +30 → 69%; inf = +18 → 18%). `aiPlanTurn` has **no hand lookahead**: it
  cannot hold a card for a better moment, only fail to pick it. There is no "hoarding".
- **`AvgSeen` ≈ `CARD_KEEP`.** The deep-hoard tail is exactly the two highest burn-reluctance values.
  **Attack +1 is not dead — it is precious**; `+1` is the deck's only button turning a
  mutual-destruction tie into a clean kill. The batch would have cut both copies.
- **The batch would have made every metric it targeted worse.** Its funding cut stranded 2 infantry
  (there is **no deploy fallback**), sending Drag 2.3 → **3.6**, attacks −20%.
- **A control deck proved the marquee mechanism inert.** Swapping the "clever" `tieSpare + noAdvance`
  attack for a plain `{attack}` changed nothing inside noise. The flags did no work at all.

**Auto-play cannot be fixed by card design — it relocates.** Split Deploy Cavalry (1stSight 79 → 22) and
**Deploy Artillery walks straight into the vacated slot: 69 → 77%.** The reflex band belongs to whoever
holds the top eval delta. This is an **AI lever**, and no card batch will ever win it.

**Iterations 2–3 — what actually worked, and one theory I had to retract.** Splitting Deploy Cavalry is
free (cavalry has `sup: 0`, so two cavalry never supported each other — the double-deploy was tempo, not
combined arms) and improves seven metrics, but costs 4 points of first-mover. Iteration 3 tried to buy
those back with opening tempo (Conscription as the guaranteed opener) and made it **worse** (40, HQ at
the 10% floor).

> **Retraction.** From those four decks I claimed first-mover% tracks HQ% monotonically. **The
> `raid-paid` deck breaks it**: identical HQ (17%) and identical battle length (31.9), yet first-mover
> recovers **42 → 46**. HQ% was a *correlate*, not the cause. The real driver is **whether tempo can be
> cashed for decisive action** — and Raiding Party provides a second way to cash it: it is a
> `deploy + attack` card, and there is **no summoning sickness**, so the unit placed in step 1 can strike
> in step 2. The first mover benefits most, because it acts first.

**The paid deck.** Raiding Party oversubscribes infantry (8 printed steps vs stock 7) and the checker's
warning was exact — at n=700, **Deploy Infantry Noop 26%, Airdrop 20%.** Paying for that 8th step (drop
Airdrop, spend the freed slot on a 3rd Attack +1) erases the dead tail (**26% → 1%**) and lands a deck
that passes T0 on every axis. Cost: the deck loses **Airdrop**, the only `anywhere` deploy — a unique
decision, and a genuinely reactive tool. That is the trade.

### The feels-match — and the finding that outranks every card

Seed 1001, haiku v haiku, first-to-3. **Blue swept 3–0. All three battles: attrition, 33 turns (the cap),
zero HQ captures.** (Hard-AI baseline: 22% HQ, 28.8 turns — the LLMs grind far harder than the AI does.)
Rush-luck check clean; fallback rate **5.8%** (13/225), a shade over the ≲5% health line — a mild caveat
on the felt-notes, not a poisoned read.

Both players independently reported the **same** strategic discovery:

> **Blue (won 3–0):** *"Current rules heavily favor whoever saves Infantry/Cavalry reserves for turn 15+."*
> … *"Red was outdeployed in all three games."* The winning pattern: *"fall back, deploy aggressively,
> grind to endgame."*
>
> **Red (lost 0–3):** *"I built 5-2 VP leads in all three battles but couldn't adapt or sustain them."*
> … *"early VP leads feel hollow."*

**The hard AI believes the exact opposite.** `unitOnBoard: 22` vs `unitReserve: 16` says a unit on the
board always beats a unit in reserve — deploy now, always. Two independent human-proxy players, playing
the actual game, found that **holding reserve and deploying late is what wins.**

**Both AI sides share the error, so it cancels in AI-vs-AI and the sweep looks perfectly healthy.** That
is why 4,200 hard-vs-hard battles could not see it, and one LLM match could. It also retro-explains the
whole run: the AI rushes its units out → the board fills → the battle grinds → attrition decides → and
attrition favours whoever still has reserve (and the second mover). The "second-mover advantage" and the
"drag" we have been chasing with cards may both be **downstream of an AI heuristic that is simply wrong.**

## 5. Suggestions — ranked, all filed for Bill

### 5a. AI levers ← **the leverage is here**

1. **`unitReserve` vs `unitOnBoard` encodes a losing strategy. Fix this first.** The LLM match is direct
   evidence that late deployment wins while the AI's eval mandates the opposite. Narrowing the gap
   (`unitReserve` 16 → 19–20, or making it urgency-scaled so reserve is *more* valuable early and less
   late) should simultaneously (a) make the AI play the actually-winning strategy, (b) compress the reflex
   band that has been contaminating every card metric, and (c) possibly dissolve the second-mover
   advantage and the drag we have been attacking with cards. **Verification recipe:** must clear the
   WOA-012 beat-hard matchup gate, then re-run the full sweep. **Hazard to respect:** tuning the AI to
   flatter the balance metrics *moves the ruler, not the game* — this one is justified because an
   *independent population* says the heuristic is wrong, not because the metrics would look nicer.
   Type: **AI**.
2. **`aiPlanTurn` has no hand lookahead — either give it one, or stop reading its card metrics as design
   signal.** The cheap half is done (the rubric now flags 1stSight/AvgSeen as AI diagnostics, never
   targets). The expensive half is real planning. Type: **AI**.
3. **`CARD_KEEP` is doing unexamined balance work** — it alone separates the hoard band (keep 3–5 →
   AvgSeen 3.8–5.7) from the deep-hoard tail (keep 8–9 → AvgSeen 7–11). Hand-authored, never reviewed.
   Type: **AI**.

### 5b. Suggested RULE / design changes

1. **The reserve-vs-board economy is a *rules* question, not just an AI one.** Both LLMs proposed the same
   class of fix unprompted — Blue: *"cap total deployments per side, or add an entrenchment tax
   (deployments cost a card action), to reward early positioning over late-game recovery."* If late
   deployment is dominant for *humans* too, the AI fix (§5a.1) will not be enough. **Measure first**
   (fix the AI, re-run, see if the pathology survives), then rule. Type: **rules**.
2. **The `starting: true` card is a live balance lever nobody was treating as one.** One flag moved
   first-mover 42 → 40, HQ 17 → 10, battle length +1.8 turns. It deserves a rubric line and deliberate
   tuning. Type: **rules/data**.
3. **Rule on the 16-card ceiling — it is the only thing blocking the adopt deck.** Measured: the cavalry
   split is clean at 17 and expensive at 16 (every 16-card funding cut broke something). This is the first
   hard price tag on that physical-board guardrail. Given the Steam target is a roguelite deck-builder
   with a pool larger than the deck, it is going to be renegotiated anyway. Type: **design decision
   (Bill)**.
4. **Re-examine The Void — its defect is geometric and no card can reach it.** It is a **donut**: hexes
   `(-1,0)` and `(0,0)` are absent from its `shapeDef`, so the HQs are split by a hole and the front is
   two narrow arms. Fewer adjacencies → fewer *legal* attacks (Atk 4.5 vs a fleet 6.1; Drag 3.7 vs a 2.5
   ceiling). And the AI is not being timid — instrumented on The Void it commits attackers into outright
   **losses 44% of the time**. It has nowhere to fight from, not cold feet. Either fix the shape or cut it
   from `core7`. Type: **content (map)**.

### 5c. Stats to gather or drop

1. **DROP per-card `Win%`.** Dead at n=700 — all 13 cards read 49–52 against a ±8 rubric threshold. It
   invites exactly the wrong grading instinct. Keep it in the DB; stop printing it.
2. **DEMOTE `1stSight%` / `AvgSeen` from design targets to AI diagnostics** — done in the rubric. They are
   readouts of `evalState` and `CARD_KEEP`.
3. **GATHER: a deploy-step-budget-vs-stock assertion in `test.js`.** The single highest-value one-liner in
   this report. Oversubscription is invisible until simulated and it silently produces 26% dead turns;
   undersubscription strands units and cannot be recovered (no deploy fallback).
4. **GATHER: reserve-held-at-end-of-battle, per side.** The LLM match's central claim ("saving reserve for
   turn 15+ wins") is currently unmeasurable from any report we produce. If §5a.1 is right, this is the
   metric that proves it.

## 6. Measurements audit

- **4,200 battles** across 6 slots, hard-vs-hard, `--once` fixed seeds, `core7`, rules 1.1. Every slot was
  step-budget-verified against stock **before** running, so no comparison is confounded by budget — except
  `cavsplit17-raid`, which was run *deliberately* oversubscribed to measure the dead-tail cost.
- **Every headline claim is a ≥700-battle number** (±3.8 pts). The checkers' exploratory 280/420-battle
  runs were **re-measured at n=700 and held** (cavalry split 79→21 ≈ 79→22; Forced March swap collapse 2.7
  ≈ 2.7; Raiding Party noop 26% ≈ 26%).
- **Two claims in this report's own earlier drafts were retracted after measurement** — "Attack +1 is the
  deadest card" (it is the most precious) and "first-mover tracks HQ%" (HQ was a correlate). Both
  retractions are recorded in §3 rather than quietly edited out.
- **Feels-match caveat:** n=1 match, 3 battles, fallback 5.8% (just over the ≲5% health line). The
  reserve-timing finding is a *hypothesis with a mechanism*, not a measured result — §5c.4 is how to
  measure it.
- **Not measured:** skill premium (hard>normal 60% / hard>easy 78%), never re-run under 1.1. Nothing here
  leans on it.
- **Confound to respect:** cutting a high-eval deploy card raises every *surviving* deploy card's 1stSight
  through crowd-out alone. Never read a 1stSight delta as a card's own doing.
