## Match felt-notes

### red (haiku)

The match showed **clear adaptation**: early wins relied on cavalry escalation and aggressive HQ positioning (battles 1-2), but when Blue shifted to attrition-focused defenses (3-5), I pivoted to careful VP trading and forward positioning. Battles 3-4 taught me that passive reserve play loses to Blue's firepower; battle 5 proved aggressive board presence wins endgames. 

**Strong**: unit positioning for support multiplication, willingness to trade units if maintaining VP leads. **Weak**: underestimating trench blocking on adjacent hexes; cavalry over-reliance early.

**Suggested change**: Reduce the gap between attack and defense stats slightly (e.g., infantry defense 1→2, cavalry defense 0→1) to make attrition battles reward positioning over card variance and give non-attack cards more viability in endgames.

### blue (haiku)

**Match retrospective (5 battles, Red 3-2):**

Adaptation mattered more than luck. Battles 1-2 showed poor early positioning (overextended Cavalry, defensive passivity); by battle 3, Blue learned attrition math and VP-parity hunting, winning 2 straight by understanding that second-player ties are winnable. Battle 4's breakthrough (HQ capture via Attack +1) proved tactical depth existed. Battle 5 showed the weakness: reaching a mathematically winning position (5-5 VP, more cards, second-player advantage) but losing to a sudden HQ capture, suggesting late-game clustering becomes tactically vulnerable.

**Strong:** Attrition endgame calculation, defensive consolidation, card advantage leverage.  
**Weak:** Early-game unit placement and over-commitment.

**Suggestion:** Require HQ captures to happen on a *win* (not a tie), not just equal power—it's too easy for late-game unit density to stumble into instant defeat.
