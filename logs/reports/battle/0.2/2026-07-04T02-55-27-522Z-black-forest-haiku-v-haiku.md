## Felt-notes

### red (haiku)

**FEEL NOTES**

**Strong:** Attrition mechanics felt tense and deliberate—trench placement and Artillery tiles created real decision points. Naval Barrage removing E2's trench was satisfying board control. Tie-trades (T7, T13, T23) created meaningful "spend a unit to advance the front" moments.

**Weak:** Too many dead-card turns (T8, T20, T31: "finds no opening"). Turn 21's Forced March was circular repositioning—not engaging. The long tail (turns 25–32) devolved into simple marches with no clear strategic direction, just waiting for deck to run out.

**Luck-driven:** Card draw killed momentum; Blue whiffed twice early (T8, T20) yet still won. Early cavalry trades felt random, set the whole match's tempo.

**Suggested change:** **Prevent dead-card whiffs.** If a card can't resolve, let it default to a basic Attack or Reposition rather than disappearing. This keeps the deck-burn cost (each card removed permanently) but makes every turn *feel* like progress, reducing the frustration of wasted plays.

### blue (haiku)

**How the game felt:**

**Strong:** Early cavalry trades were visceral and decisive. Trenches provided meaningful defense in the grinding endgame. Through-HQ attacks added tactical depth when units clustered around the center.

**Weak:** Multiple dead-hand skips (T8, T20, T31) felt frustrating—the deck's card variance created long stretches where neither side had meaningful options. The late game devolved into repositioning shuffles (Forced March used just to burn a card).

**Luck-driven:** Cavalry opening trades hinged entirely on which support units were adjacent; positioning luck dominated turns 2–7. The attrition win came down to who happened to have units left standing—felt thin on player agency.

**One change:** Allow cards that can't resolve to be *returned to hand* (or reshuffled immediately) instead of simply discarded as no-ops. Dead hands kill momentum and make the endgame feel like a war of attrition against the deck, not the opponent.
